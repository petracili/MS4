Sempervivum tectorum

It grows in the mountains of western, central and southern Europe. There are also a large number of cultivated traits, and it has been grown as an ornamental plant for about 200 years. It is medicinal and the young leaves are edible. The Sempervivum tectorum originally grew in southern European rocky areas, and its distribution was due to Charlemagne, who considered it medicinal and ordered it to be planted for its healing properties so that the plant spread throughout Europe. The flower stalk, which grows 10 to 30 centimetres, is covered with small fleshy pointed leaves at the top, which are said to be medicinal. The leaves are collected from the beginning of March to the end of October, and by pressing them the juice (Sempervivi tectori succus) is obtained.

Streptocarpus sect. Saintpaulia

Streptocarpus sect. Saintpaulia is a section within Streptocarpus subgenus Streptocarpella[1] consisting of about ten species of herbaceous perennial flowering plants in the family Gesneriaceae, native to Tanzania and adjacent southeastern Kenya in eastern tropical Africa. The section was previously treated as a separate genus, Saintpaulia, but molecular phylogenetic studies showed that it was nested within the genus Streptocarpus. Species and cultivars are commonly called African violets (although they are not closely related to true violets) or saintpaulias. They are commonly used as indoor houseplants, but can also be kept as outdoor plants in certain climates. Several of the species and subspecies of the section are endangered, and many more are threatened, as their native cloud forest habitats are cleared for agriculture. The conservation status of Streptocarpus ionanthus has been classed as near-threatened.

Dracaena fragrans

Dracaena fragrans is a slow growing shrub, usually multistemmed at the base, mature specimens reaching 15 m (49 ft) or more tall with a narrow crown of usually slender erect branches. Stems may reach up to 30 cm (12 in) diameter on old plants; in forest habitats they may become horizontal with erect side branches. Young plants have a single unbranched stem with a rosette of leaves until the growing tip flowers or is damaged, after which it branches, producing two or more new stems; thereafter, branching increases with subsequent flowering episodes.
